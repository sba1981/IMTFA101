Sessions 1 – 3  ·  ICAI Framework

Framework for Preparation &
Presentation of Financial Statements

The ICAI framework provides the conceptual foundation for accounting standards — covering what financial statements are, who uses them, and what makes them useful.

1. Purpose of the Framework

The Accounting Standards Board (ASB) of the ICAI issued the Framework in July 2000. It provides the fundamental basis for developing new accounting standards and reviewing existing ones.

The framework assists:

Enterprises
Prepare statements in compliance with accounting standards
ASB
Develop and review accounting standards consistently
Auditors
Form opinions on whether statements conform to standards
Users
Interpret financial statements for economic decisions
⚠️ Important In case of conflict between an Accounting Standard and the Framework, the requirements of the Accounting Standard prevail.

2. Components of Financial Statements

A complete set of financial statements consists of four parts

📊

Balance Sheet

Portrays the value of economic resources controlled by an enterprise. Provides information about liquidity and solvency — useful in predicting the ability to meet financial commitments.

Position Statement
📈

Statement of Profit & Loss

Presents the result of operations for an accounting period. Shows revenue earned, expenses incurred, and the resulting profit or loss.

Flow Statement
💵

Cash Flow Statement

Shows how an enterprise has generated cash (inflows) and how it has been used (outflows) during an accounting period across operating, investing, and financing activities.

Flow Statement
📝

Notes & Schedules

Present supplementary information explaining items in the financial statements — including disclosures about risks, uncertainties, accounting policies, and segment reports.

Supplementary
📌 Stock vs Flow The Balance Sheet is a stock measure — it captures position at a point in time. The P&L and Cash Flow statements are flow measures — they capture activity over a period.

3. Objectives & Users of Financial Statements

The objective of financial statements is to provide information about the financial position, performance, and cash flows of an enterprise that is useful to a wide range of users for making economic decisions.

Seven Broad Groups of Users

💼

Investors

Assess risk and return to decide whether to buy, hold, or sell their investment.

👷

Employees

Interested in stability of the enterprise, ability to pay salaries and retirement benefits.

🏦

Lenders

Determine whether loans and related interest will be paid when due.

🛒

Suppliers & Creditors

Assess whether amounts owed to them will be paid when they fall due.

🤝

Customers

Interested in continuity of the enterprise, especially for long-term contracts or warranties.

🏛️

Government

Regulate enterprises, assess taxes, compile national statistics, and set policy.

🌍

Public

Enterprises affect the community through employment, local suppliers, and environmental impact.

4. Fundamental Accounting Assumptions

Users take these for granted — no separate disclosure needed unless one is not followed

Going Concern

Financial statements are prepared on the assumption that an enterprise will continue in operation in the foreseeable future. There is neither intention nor need to materially curtail the scale of operations.

Under this assumption, assets are recorded at cost (not liquidation value) and liabilities are expected to be settled in the normal course of business.

📌 Key Implication If an enterprise is being wound up or is at risk of closure, it must disclose that going concern has NOT been assumed and explain the basis used instead.

Accrual Basis

Transactions are recognised when they occur, not when cash is received or paid. Revenue is recognised when earned; expenses when incurred.

Accrual vs Cash Basis — Quick Comparison

Situation Cash Basis Accrual Basis
Credit sale in Period 1, cash received in Period 2Recognised in Period 2Recognised in Period 1
Salary for December paid in JanuaryRecognised in JanuaryRecognised in December
Advance received for services not yet renderedIncome when receivedLiability until service delivered
ℹ️ Why Accrual is Better Accrual basis ensures better matching between revenue and cost. Section 209(3)(b) of the Companies Act makes it mandatory for companies to maintain accounts on an accrual basis.

Consistency

The same accounting policies should be applied for similar transactions across all accounting periods. This improves the comparability of financial statements over time.

An accounting policy can be changed only if required by:

A Statute or Law An Accounting Standard For More Appropriate Presentation
📌 Disclosure Rule Any change in accounting policy must be disclosed, along with its effect on the financial statements.

5. Qualitative Characteristics

Attributes that make financial statement information useful to its users

🧠

Understandability

Information must be presented in a manner readily understandable by users with reasonable knowledge of business and economic activities. More information is not always better — irrelevant information creates confusion.

🎯

Relevance

Information that influences the economic decisions of users is relevant. It may help evaluate past, present, or future events, or confirm/correct past evaluations. Relevance is judged by materiality — omission or misstatement of material information can mislead users.

✅

Reliability

Information must be free from material error and bias. To be reliable, it must:
• Faithfully represent transactions
• Reflect economic substance over legal form
• Be neutral and free from bias
• Exercise prudence in uncertain outcomes

🔄

Comparability

Users must be able to compare financial statements across time (same enterprise) and across enterprises. Consistent application of accounting policies enables meaningful comparisons. Accounting policies must be disclosed to allow users to understand the basis.

🤝

Faithful Representation

Financial statements must faithfully represent transactions and other events they purport to represent. The economic substance of a transaction must be reflected even if the legal form differs (substance over form).

6. Elements of Financial Statements

The framework identifies five broad elements directly related to the measurement of financial position and performance:

Assets

Resources controlled by the enterprise as a result of past events, from which future economic benefits are expected to flow.

Balance Sheet

Liabilities

Present obligations arising from past events, the settlement of which is expected to result in an outflow of economic benefits.

Balance Sheet

Equity

The residual interest in the assets of the enterprise after deducting all its liabilities. Also called net assets or owners' equity.

Balance Sheet

Income (Revenue)

Increases in economic benefits during an accounting period in the form of inflows or enhancements of assets or decreases in liabilities that result in increases in equity.

P&L Statement

Expenses

Decreases in economic benefits during an accounting period in the form of outflows or depletions of assets or incurrences of liabilities that result in decreases in equity.

P&L Statement

Session 4  ·  The Accounting Equation

The Foundation of
Double-Entry Accounting

Every business transaction affects the accounting equation. The equation always holds — this is the bedrock of financial accounting.

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Liabilities + Equity

Every transaction changes at least two items — but the equation always remains balanced.

Left Side — What the business OWNS

Assets

Resources controlled by the enterprise from which future economic benefits are expected. Examples: cash, bank, equipment, receivables, prepaid deposits.

=

Right Side — Who FUNDED those assets

L + E

Liabilities — what the business owes to outsiders (bank loans, creditors, accrued expenses).

Equity — the owner's claim; capital contributed plus retained profits.

📌 Why It Always Balances Assets are always financed by someone — either creditors (liabilities) or the owner (equity). Every ₹ of assets must have an equal and opposite source of funding.

The Expanded Equation

When we include income and expenses (which affect equity), the equation expands to:

Assets = Liabilities + Capital + Income − Expenses

Income increases equity; Expenses decrease equity.

Capital (Owner's Contribution)
Increases Equity ↑
Revenue Earned
Increases Equity ↑
Expenses Incurred
Decreases Equity ↓
Drawings / Dividends
Decreases Equity ↓

Key Accounting Concepts Applied in This Case

📅

Accrual Basis

Salary for May is recognised as an expense on May 31 (when earned by the employee) even though it was paid in June.

🔄

Deferred Revenue

Rebecca Olsen's advance is a liability until each service is performed. ₹50,000 is earned per service rendered.

⚖️

Matching Principle

Supplies consumed (₹24,000) are expensed in the period they are used, not when purchased — matching cost with benefit.

🏢

Going Concern

Assets like furniture and equipment are recorded at cost, not liquidation value — the firm is expected to continue operating.

Detailed Overview

Legal structure, suitability, and key features of each form

🧑‍💼 Sole Proprietorship

Min: 1 Max: 1 Unlimited Liability

The simplest and oldest form of business organisation. The owner and business are the same legal person — there is no distinction between personal and business assets.

Governing Law
No specific Act; governed by general laws & tax regulations
Registration
Not mandatory; MSME/Shop & Establishment Act registration optional
Taxation
Income taxed as personal income of the proprietor
Continuity
Ceases on owner's death or insolvency — no perpetual succession
✅ Best Suited For Small retail shops, street vendors, local service providers, freelancers, and artisans. Ideal when simplicity, low cost, and full control matter more than liability protection. Examples: a local kirana store, a tuition teacher, a small tailor shop.

🏠 Hindu Undivided Family (HUF)

Min: 2 Max: No limit Karta: Unlimited

A unique form recognised only under Indian (Hindu) law. The family as a unit carries on business. The senior-most male member (or female, post-2005) is the Karta who manages the business; other members are coparceners.

Governing Law
Hindu Succession Act, 1956; Hindu Law (Mitakshara / Dayabhaga schools)
Registration
Not required; PAN registration with Income Tax Department needed
Taxation
Taxed separately as a distinct assessee under Income Tax Act — key tax advantage
Membership
Automatic — all Hindu lineal descendants of a common ancestor; wives & daughters included post-2005
✅ Best Suited For Traditional family businesses in agriculture, trading, and manufacturing. Widely used for tax planning — HUF income is taxed separately, effectively splitting the family's tax burden. Examples: agricultural land cultivation, ancestral jewellery businesses.

🤝 Partnership Firm

Min: 2 Max: 50 Unlimited Liability

Two or more persons agree to share profits of a business carried on by all or any one of them acting for all. Each partner is an agent of the firm and of fellow partners. Liability is joint and several — a creditor can sue any one partner for the entire debt.

Governing Law
Indian Partnership Act, 1932
Registration
Optional but strongly advisable — unregistered firms cannot sue third parties
Max Members
50 (Companies Act 2013, Sec 464); banking partnerships capped at 10
Taxation
Firm taxed at flat 30% + surcharge; partners' share exempt in their hands
✅ Best Suited For Small and medium trading businesses, professional practices (doctor clinics, consultancies), and family-run enterprises where partners bring complementary skills. Examples: a law firm, a medical clinic, a small manufacturing unit.

⚖️ Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

Min: 2 Designated Partners Max: No limit Limited Liability

A hybrid form combining the flexibility of a partnership with the limited liability of a company. Each partner's liability is limited to their agreed contribution. Partners are not liable for the wrongful acts of other partners — a key advantage over ordinary partnership.

Governing Law
Limited Liability Partnership Act, 2008
Designated Partners
Min 2; at least one must be a resident Indian; responsible for regulatory compliance
Taxation
Taxed like a partnership firm — 30% flat rate; no dividend distribution tax
Separate Legal Entity
Yes — LLP can own property, sue & be sued in its own name
✅ Best Suited For Professional service firms — CA firms, Company Secretaries, law firms, architecture firms, management consultancies, and startups seeking limited liability without heavy company compliance. ICAI mandates CA firms with more than 2 partners to register as LLPs.

👤 One Person Company (OPC)

Min: 1 Max: 1 Shareholder Limited Liability

Introduced by the Companies Act 2013 to give solo entrepreneurs the benefits of a company structure. Requires a single shareholder and one nominee (who takes over if the shareholder dies or becomes incapacitated). Minimum 1 director required.

Governing Law
Companies Act, 2013 — Section 2(62)
Eligibility
Only natural persons who are Indian citizens & residents; NRIs allowed since April 2021
Conversion
Must convert to Private Ltd once paid-up capital exceeds ₹50 lakhs or turnover exceeds ₹2 crores
Taxation
Taxed as a company — base rate 22% (for existing cos) or 25% (new cos under Sec 115BAB)
✅ Best Suited For Solo entrepreneurs and freelancers who want corporate credibility, limited liability, and easier access to institutional credit — without taking on a co-founder. Examples: a solo IT consultant, a single-person e-commerce seller, an independent financial advisor.

🏢 Private Limited Company

Min: 2 Members, 2 Directors Max: 200 Members Limited Liability

The most popular form for startups and growing businesses. Restricts the right to transfer shares and prohibits public invitation to subscribe to shares. Has a separate legal identity from its owners — the company can own property, enter contracts, and continue to exist regardless of changes in ownership.

Governing Law
Companies Act, 2013 — Section 2(68)
Directors
Min 2, Max 15 (can be increased by special resolution); at least 1 resident Indian director
Share Transfer
Restricted — must be approved by existing shareholders; cannot be listed on a stock exchange
Compliance
Annual ROC filings, audited accounts, board meetings mandatory; less disclosure than public company
✅ Best Suited For Tech startups, family businesses seeking growth capital, subsidiaries of foreign companies, and any enterprise planning VC/PE funding. The preferred structure for investors because equity can be issued, transferred, and pledged easily. Examples: Flipkart (early stage), Zomato (pre-IPO), most Indian IT startups.

🏛️ Public Limited Company

Min: 7 Members, 3 Directors Max: No limit Limited Liability

Can invite the general public to subscribe to its shares and debentures. Shares are freely transferable. Must add "Limited" or "Ltd" to its name. Listed public companies are regulated additionally by SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India).

Governing Law
Companies Act, 2013; SEBI Act, 1992 (for listed companies)
Share Transfer
Freely transferable; can be listed on BSE / NSE for public trading
Capital Raising
Can issue IPO, FPO, bonds & debentures to the general public
Disclosure
High disclosure requirements — quarterly results, annual reports, SEBI filings mandatory for listed companies
✅ Best Suited For Large enterprises requiring substantial capital from the public, companies planning stock exchange listing, and businesses with broad public stakeholder interest. Offers maximum credibility and access to capital markets. Examples: Reliance Industries Ltd, Tata Consultancy Services Ltd, HDFC Bank Ltd.

🌾 Co-operative Society

Min: 10 Members Max: Per bye-laws Limited Liability

Voluntary association of persons working together for mutual economic benefit. Based on the principle of "one member, one vote" — regardless of the number of shares held. Surplus is distributed as dividends in proportion to members' participation (patronage), not share capital.

Governing Law
Co-operative Societies Act, 1912; State Co-operative Acts; Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002
Registration
Mandatory with the Registrar of Co-operative Societies of the respective state
Management
Elected management committee; democratic control — each member has one vote irrespective of shares
Surplus Distribution
Distributed as dividends & patronage refunds; portion compulsorily transferred to reserves
✅ Best Suited For Agricultural credit (PACS — Primary Agricultural Credit Societies), dairy cooperatives, housing societies, consumer stores, and weaver cooperatives. Where collective bargaining, shared resources, and mutual benefit matter more than profit maximisation. Examples: AMUL (Gujarat Co-operative Milk Marketing Federation), Saraswat Co-operative Bank, DDA Housing Cooperatives.

Key Distinctions at a Glance

⚠️

Unlimited vs Limited Liability

Sole proprietorships, partnerships, and HUF Kartas face unlimited personal liability — creditors can attach personal assets. LLPs, companies, and OPCs protect personal assets beyond the invested capital.

📜

Separate Legal Entity

Only companies (Pvt Ltd, Public Ltd, OPC) and LLPs are separate legal entities. A partnership firm and sole proprietorship are NOT separate from their owners in the eyes of the law.

♾️

Perpetual Succession

Companies and LLPs continue to exist irrespective of changes in ownership or the death of a member. Proprietorships and partnerships dissolve on the owner's/partner's death unless the partnership deed provides otherwise.

💰

Capital Raising Ability

Public Limited Companies have the greatest ability to raise capital (IPO, bonds, public deposits). Private Ltd comes next (VC/PE). Proprietorships and HUFs are largely limited to owner funds and bank loans.
